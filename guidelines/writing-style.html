Writing style, tone & voice
How RDC communicates — in client deliverables, internal documents, social content, and everything in between.

Voice & character

RDC's voice is the consistent personality behind everything we write. It doesn't change based on the audience — only the tone shifts. Think of voice as who we are, and tone as how we show up in a given moment.

Red Door Collaborative writes plainly and with confidence that doesn't need to announce itself. We sound like people who know what they're doing — not like a press release, a corporate deck, or an LLM.

Conversational, not corporate

We use plain language. We use contractions. We sound like a person, not an institution. If a sentence could appear in a funding announcement from a company no one's heard of, rewrite it.

Confident without being self-promotional

We let the work speak. "We helped solve this problem" is stronger than "we revolutionized the industry." Superlatives without evidence erode credibility rather than build it.

Curious and honest

We share what we know and acknowledge what we don't. We ask questions as readily as we offer answers. Vague claims and inflated language are the fastest way to sound like everyone else.

Specific over sweeping

We say what we mean. Specifics build trust; broad claims don't. "Engagement increased 23%" is more credible than "we drove strong results." Name the thing.

What we're not

  • Not corporate-speak. Words like "leverage," "synergy," "seamless," and "robust" are red flags. Write the plain version instead. See the words and phrases section for a full replacement list.
  • Not passive. We don't hide behind passive constructions. "RDC designed the framework" — not "the framework was designed."
  • Not AI-generated. As a creative agency, sounding like generated text is a particular liability. Uniform sentence length, overuse of headers, filler transitions, and significance inflation are all detectable tells. Avoid them.

Tone by context

Our voice is consistent — our tone adapts. Here's how to calibrate for each context.

Context Tone Example
Client deliverable Confident, professional, precise "This quarter, engagement increased 23% — driven primarily by the LinkedIn content sprint in March."
Project brief / SOW Clear, structured, direct "RDC will deliver three assets: a revised style guide, a social content calendar, and a 60-day campaign brief."
Social media Warm, conversational, human "We believe the best brands are built from the inside out — starting with the people who show up every day."
Internal documentation Practical, direct, low ceremony "Use the file name builder before saving anything. The SOP is the source of truth."
Outreach / proposals Engaged, curious, collaborative "We'd love to learn more about what you're building — and share what we've seen work in similar organizations."

Names & capitalization

Agency name

Red Door Collaborative on first reference in any document. Red Door on subsequent references within that document. RDC is internal shorthand — use it in Teams chats and internal notes, but not in external documents, RFPs, proposals, or anything a client or prospect will read.

Internal / informal
  • RDC brand guidelines (Teams message)
  • RDC submitted the proposal (internal note)
External / formal
  • Red Door Collaborative is pleased to… (first reference)
  • Red Door's approach to… (subsequent reference)

Team names and titles

Capitalize proper names and full titles. Don't abbreviate what you wouldn't abbreviate in a formal context.

  • Named teams are capitalized: Creative Team, not "creative team"
  • Titles are spelled out: Senior, not Sr.; Manager, not Mgr.

Headings and document titles

Title case only for the titles of documents and deck titles on the first slide. Sentence case for all headings — capitalize the first word and proper nouns only.

Writing rules

Use contractions

Contractions make writing sound like a person wrote it. Use we're, we've, it's, don't, isn't, etc. Avoiding contractions in an attempt to sound professional has the opposite effect.

Vary sentence length deliberately

Short sentences land hard. Longer ones can carry more nuance and context, but two long sentences back-to-back slow everything down. Mix them up. A single short sentence after a long one can do more work than three more long ones.

If every paragraph is roughly the same length, that's a structural tell — and one of the clearest signals of AI-generated writing. Break up the pattern.

Use headers intentionally

Headers are navigation aids. Use them when a reader genuinely needs to find their way through a long or complex document. Don't use them to break up text that flows naturally as prose. If a document has more headers than it has paragraphs, that's a signal something's off.

Active voice

Put the actor first. Active voice is shorter, clearer, and more direct.

Write this
  • RDC designed the framework.
  • We increased engagement by 40%.
  • The team delivered on time.
Not this
  • The framework was designed by RDC.
  • Engagement was increased by 40%.
  • The delivery was completed on time.

Lead with the result

In client-facing writing especially, put the most important thing first. Don't build to a conclusion — state it, then support it.

Write this

LinkedIn content drove the strongest growth this quarter — follower count increased 18% and post reach doubled year-over-year, fueled by a shift toward employee-voice storytelling.

Not this

This quarter we tried several different content approaches across multiple platforms, and after reviewing the data for each, we determined that LinkedIn performed best, showing an 18% follower increase.

Don't overpromise or oversell

We've done great work. We don't need to inflate it. Superlatives without evidence, "game-changing" claims, and phrases like "revolutionized the industry" erode credibility rather than build it. Say what actually happened.

Numbers and data

  • Spell out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
  • Percentages: numeral + % symbol, always. (47%, not "forty-seven percent" or "47 percent.")
  • Don't start a sentence with a numeral. Spell it out or rewrite the sentence.
Write this
  • Seventeen clients joined in Q3.
  • In Q3, 17 clients joined.
  • Engagement increased 47%.
Not this
  • 17 clients joined in Q3.
  • forty-seven percent increase
  • 47 percent increase

Punctuation

Default to AP style. The items below are the decisions that come up most often.

Oxford comma

No Oxford comma. In a series of three or more items, no comma before the final "and" or "or."

Write this
  • We write for enterprises, startups and agencies.
  • Brand, content and communications.
Not this
  • We write for enterprises, startups, and agencies.
  • Brand, content, and communications.

Em-dashes and en-dashes

  • Em dash (—): use sparingly — roughly one per several paragraphs as a ceiling. Overuse is one of the most common signals of AI-generated writing. No spaces around em-dashes.
  • En dash (–): for ranges: 2020–2024, pages 12–18, Q1–Q3.
  • Don't use a hyphen (-) where a dash is called for.

Bullet punctuation and parallel structure

Complete sentences in bullets take a period. Fragments do not. All items in a list must be grammatically parallel — same structure, same part of speech leading each item. Don't mix sentences and fragments in the same list.

Commas with coordinating conjunctions

Use a comma before and, but, or, and so when they join two independent clauses.

Quotation marks

Periods and commas always go inside the closing quotation mark.

Write this

He called it "the clearest brief we've received."

Not this

He called it "the clearest brief we've received".

Compound modifiers

Hyphenate compound modifiers that precede a noun. Don't hyphenate when they follow one.

Before the noun
  • a data-driven strategy
  • a well-crafted brief
After the noun
  • the strategy is data driven
  • the brief was well crafted

Possessives

Add 's to singular nouns, including those ending in s. Plural possessives take the apostrophe after the s.

  • the agency's voice
  • Marcus's feedback
  • the writers' guide

Ampersands

Use "&" only in proper names, navigation labels, and UI where space is limited. Spell out "and" in body copy.

Structural phrases: cut entirely

These phrases add length without meaning. Cut them and start with the point.

Filler openers — cut and lead with the idea

  • "In today's fast-paced world…"
  • "In an era where…"
  • "Let's explore…" / "Let's dive into…"
  • "In this article, we will…"

Significance inflation — just state the thing

  • "cannot be overstated"
  • "profound impact"
  • "plays a crucial role"
  • "marking a pivotal moment"

Transitional filler — restructure or cut

  • "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally"
  • "It's worth noting that" / "Notably"
  • "That being said"
  • "At the end of the day"

Generic closers — say something specific, or just end

  • "The future looks bright"
  • "Only time will tell"
  • "As we move forward"
  • "This is just the beginning"

Contrastive negation

Strictly avoid "it's not X, it's Y" and "not just X, it's Y" constructions. Rewrite to say the thing directly.

Write this

Good content builds connection.

Not this

"This isn't just about content — it's about connection."

AI formatting tells

Beyond word choice, certain structural patterns signal AI generation. Avoid them:

  • Uniform sentence length. Vary it deliberately — mix short sentences with longer ones or use the occasional fragment.
  • The intro-list-conclusion template. Announce what you're about to say, say it, summarize what you said — this is a dead giveaway. Lead with the point and write with a through-line.
  • Overuse of bold headers on short documents. One of the clearest AI formatting signals. Use headers for navigation only.
  • Every paragraph the same length. That's structural uniformity. Break up the pattern.
