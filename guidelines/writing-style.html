Writing style, tone & voice
How RDC communicates — in client deliverables, internal documents, social content, and everything in between.

Voice & character

RDC's voice is the consistent personality behind everything we write. It doesn't change based on the audience — only the tone shifts. Think of voice as who we are, and tone as how we show up in a given moment.

How we sound

Direct

We say what we mean without hedging. Clear sentences over long-winded qualifications. We respect the reader's time.

Warm

We're people working with people. We write like a trusted colleague — honest, supportive, never clinical or transactional.

Credible

We back our claims. We don't oversell or use superlatives. Our expertise shows through precision, not performance.

What we're not

  • Not corporate-speak. Avoid buzzwords like "leverage," "synergy," or "best-in-class." Write the plain version instead.
  • Not casual to the point of sloppiness. Warmth doesn't mean typos, sentence fragments, or emoji in professional documents.
  • Not passive. We don't hide behind passive constructions. "RDC designed the framework" — not "the framework was designed."

Tone by context

Our voice is consistent — our tone adapts. Here's how to calibrate for each context.

Context Tone Example
Client deliverable Confident, professional, precise "This quarter, engagement increased 23% — driven primarily by the LinkedIn content sprint in March."
Project brief / SOW Clear, structured, direct "RDC will deliver three assets: a revised style guide, a social content calendar, and a 60-day campaign brief."
Social media Warm, conversational, human "We believe the best brands are built from the inside out — starting with the people who show up every day."
Internal documentation Practical, direct, low ceremony "Use the file name builder before saving anything. The SOP is the source of truth."
Outreach / proposals Engaged, curious, collaborative "We'd love to learn more about what you're building — and share what we've seen work in similar organizations."

Writing rules

Sentence case for headings

We use sentence case for all headings — capitalize only the first word and proper nouns. Never title case.

Write this
  • Writing style, tone and voice
  • Social media guide
  • Executive summary
Not this
  • Writing Style, Tone And Voice
  • Social Media Guide
  • Executive Summary

Active voice

Put the actor first. Active voice is shorter, clearer, and more direct.

Write this
  • RDC designed the framework.
  • We increased engagement by 40%.
  • The team delivered on time.
Not this
  • The framework was designed by RDC.
  • Engagement was increased by 40%.
  • The delivery was completed on time.

Lead with the result

In client-facing writing especially, put the most important thing first. Don't build to a conclusion — state it, then support it.

Write this

LinkedIn content drove the strongest growth this quarter — follower count increased 18% and post reach doubled year-over-year, fueled by a shift toward employee-voice storytelling.

Not this

This quarter we tried several different content approaches across multiple platforms, and after reviewing the data for each, we determined that LinkedIn performed best, showing an 18% follower increase.

Short paragraphs

Aim for 2–4 sentences per paragraph in most contexts. A single sentence can stand alone if it earns it. Long unbroken blocks slow down even willing readers.

Oxford comma

Always use the serial (Oxford) comma before the conjunction in a list of three or more items: "brand, content, and communications."

Numbers

  • Spell out one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above.
  • Always use numerals for percentages: "4% increase," not "four percent increase."
  • Use commas in numbers 1,000 and above.
  • In data-dense contexts (tables, metrics), numerals are always fine.

Dates

  • Full dates: "June 9, 2026" — not "6/9/26" in prose.
  • Month + year: "June 2026" — no comma.
  • Date ranges: "June–August 2026" (en dash, no spaces).

Punctuation & formatting

Em dashes and en dashes

  • Em dash (—): use to set off a clause or add emphasis, with no spaces on either side: "The report — and the data behind it — speaks for itself."
  • En dash (–): use in ranges: "pages 12–18," "Q1–Q3 2026."
  • Don't use a hyphen (-) where a dash is called for.

Quotation marks

Use "curly" (smart) quotes, not "straight" quotes. Punctuation goes inside closing quotation marks in American English: "This is the approach," she said.

Ampersands

Use "&" only in proper names, navigation labels, and UI where space is limited. Spell out "and" in body copy.

Bold and italics

  • Bold for key terms on first use, UI labels, or the most important thing in a paragraph. Use sparingly — if everything is bold, nothing is.
  • Italics for titles of works, technical terms, or occasional emphasis. Not for decorative effect.

Capitalization

  • Capitalize formal proper nouns: Red Door Collaborative, Microsoft Azure, LinkedIn.
  • Lowercase generic job titles unless used directly before a name: "the account manager" but "Account Manager Brian Jensen."
  • Lowercase "the" in "the internet," "the web," "the cloud."

Avoid

  • Buzzwords and jargon: synergy, best-in-class, robust, scalable, ecosystem (overused), pivot, circle back, move the needle, low-hanging fruit.
  • Hedging phrases: "It should be noted that," "It goes without saying," "Needless to say."
  • Filler intros: "In today's fast-paced world," "Now more than ever."
  • Redundant pairs: "each and every," "first and foremost," "various and sundry."

RDC-specific terms

  • Red Door Collaborative — always spelled out on first reference per page. "RDC" is acceptable on subsequent references.
  • rdc.css / rdc.js — lowercase, monospace in documentation contexts.
  • Foundations library — sentence case. Not "Foundations Library" (title case).
