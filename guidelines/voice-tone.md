# Voice and tone

How Red Door Collaborative communicates — in writing, in conversation, and in every deliverable we produce.

> **Status:** This document is actively being developed by the content design lead. The principles below represent our current working definition. Sections marked `[in progress]` will be expanded.

## Our voice

Voice is consistent — it's who we are, regardless of context. Tone shifts based on audience and situation.

**Red Door's voice is:**

- **Direct.** We say what we mean and mean what we say. No hedging, no filler, no corporate speak.
- **Knowledgeable without being superior.** We know our work. We share what we know clearly, not to impress.
- **Human first.** We're a people-first organization. That shows in how we write — warmly, without being precious about it.
- **Precise.** We choose words deliberately. Precise language respects the reader's time.

**Red Door's voice is not:**
- Jargon-heavy or acronym-dependent
- Casually sloppy (warmth ≠ careless)
- Overly formal or institutional
- Hedging ("kind of," "sort of," "maybe," "perhaps" as filler)

## Tone by context

| Context | Tone |
|---------|------|
| Client-facing deliverables | Confident, clear, polished — warm but professional |
| Internal documentation | Direct, efficient — no need to perform |
| Presentations | Authoritative with breathing room — not dense prose |
| Sensitive topics (HR, feedback, change) | Warm, careful, plainspoken |
| Technical documentation | Precise and scannable — step-by-step when needed |

## Writing principles

### Lead with what matters most
Put the most important information first. Don't build to a conclusion — state it, then support it.

### Write for scanners
Most readers scan before they read. Use headers, short paragraphs, and clear hierarchy to reward scanning.

### Use plain language
Prefer common words over formal ones. "Use" not "utilize." "Help" not "facilitate." "Now" not "at this point in time."

### Sentence case, always
Headings, labels, buttons, nav items — all sentence case. **Never title case.**

> Correct: "Content style guidelines"
> Incorrect: "Content Style Guidelines"

### Active voice by default
Active is clearer and shorter. Use passive only when the actor is unknown or unimportant.

> Active: "The team reviewed the brief."
> Passive: "The brief was reviewed by the team."

### Numbers and data
- Spell out numbers one through nine; use numerals for 10 and above
- Use numerals for percentages (5%, not five percent)
- For large numbers: 1.2M, 48K — not 1,200,000 or 48,000 in running text

## What we avoid

| Avoid | Use instead |
|-------|-------------|
| leverage (as a verb) | use, apply |
| utilize | use |
| synergy, synergize | [be specific about what's happening] |
| circle back | follow up |
| move the needle | [be specific about what changes] |
| at the end of the day | [cut it — doesn't add meaning] |
| going forward | from now on / starting [date] |
| per my last email | [restate the point directly] |

---

## Punctuation

- Oxford comma: **always**
- Em dash (—): use for strong interruptions, not en dashes (–)
- Ellipsis: use sparingly and only for genuine omission, not casual trailing off
- Exclamation marks: one is an emphasis; three are noise. Use rarely.
- Quotation marks: double marks for direct quotes, single for titles within quotes
