Using AI at RDC
How we use AI tools — thoughtfully, transparently, and in ways that make our work better without compromising quality or client trust.

Philosophy

AI is a tool, not a replacement for judgment. At RDC, we use AI to move faster on the things that don't require our full expertise, so we can spend more time on the things that do.

The standard for everything we produce — with or without AI — is the same: it has to be accurate, on-brand, and something we'd put our name on. If AI got you 80% there but the last 20% is wrong or generic, the output isn't done yet.

Accelerate

Use AI to do the tedious and time-consuming things faster — drafts, summaries, reformatting, research synthesis.

Elevate

AI output is a starting point. Your judgment, context, and voice are what turn a draft into something worth delivering.

Protect

Client data, confidential information, and anything under NDA stays out of AI tools that train on user inputs.

Tool approval process
Before using a new AI tool on client work, confirm with Brian that the tool's data handling meets our client commitments. This is especially important for tools that offer "free" tiers — free often means your inputs train the model.

Prompt practices

The quality of AI output is almost entirely determined by the quality of the prompt. These practices consistently produce better results.

Provide role and context first

Tell the AI who it is and what the work is for before issuing a task. Generic prompts produce generic output.

Example: "You're a communications strategist writing for Red Door Collaborative, a boutique consulting firm that works with tech companies and nonprofits. Our voice is direct, warm, and credible — never corporate or jargon-heavy. Write a LinkedIn caption about [topic]."

Specify format and length

AI tools will default to a generic length and format. Tell it exactly what you want: "Write 3 bullet points," "Keep this under 100 words," "Format this as a table with columns for X, Y, and Z."

Give examples when possible

If you have a sample of the style you want, include it. "Match the tone of this example: [paste example]." This works better than describing the tone abstractly.

Iterate, don't rephrase and repeat

If the first draft isn't right, tell the AI what's wrong specifically. "The second paragraph is too formal — rewrite it to sound more conversational." Vague feedback gets vague improvements.

Useful prompt structures for common tasks

TaskPrompt structure
First draft of a document "Draft a [doc type] for [audience]. It should cover: [list topics]. Tone: [description]. Length: approximately [X] words."
Editing existing copy "Edit this for clarity and concision. Remove jargon. Keep the meaning intact. [paste copy]"
Extracting key points "Summarize the 5 most important points from this document. Use bullet points. [paste document]"
Generating options "Give me 5 different opening lines for this LinkedIn post. Each should take a different angle. [paste post context]"
Reformat for a new context "Rewrite this email as a LinkedIn post. Adjust the tone for a public audience. Keep it under 200 words."

Review & attribution

Always review AI output before use

AI can be confidently wrong. Verify any facts, statistics, dates, or specific claims against a primary source before including them in client work. AI models have knowledge cutoffs and hallucinate details.

Make it yours

AI output should be a starting point. Read it critically, edit it for voice, and add the context and nuance that only you have. If you can publish it without changing a word, you probably need to push the AI harder — or the work didn't need AI to begin with.

Attribution

RDC does not currently have a blanket disclosure policy for AI-assisted content. Use this judgment framework:

  • Client deliverables: AI-assisted drafting is fine; the final product must reflect your analysis and judgment. No disclosure required unless the client has asked specifically.
  • Proposals and SOWs: Same standard — AI can draft, but all commitments must be things we can actually deliver.
  • Social content: No disclosure required for AI-assisted writing that you've substantially edited and approved.
  • Research or analysis: Do not present AI-generated analysis as primary research. AI can help you synthesize; primary sources are the evidence.

Boundaries

These are hard rules — not guidelines, not judgment calls.

No confidential client data in free-tier AI tools
Client names, project details, financial data, unreleased strategy, and any information covered by an NDA must not be entered into any AI tool that trains on user inputs (including free-tier ChatGPT). Paid tiers with data-privacy agreements are a different question — confirm first.
Don't publish unreviewed AI output
Nothing generated by AI goes to a client or goes public without a human reading it, editing it, and signing off on it. Speed is not worth the risk of a factual error, a tone miss, or a claim we can't stand behind.
No AI-generated images of real people without consent
Do not use AI image tools to generate likenesses of clients, partners, or colleagues. This applies to deepfakes, stylized portraits, and any synthetic media involving identifiable individuals.

When to push back on AI

AI tools are optimistic by nature — they'll attempt any task and deliver something that looks complete. Be skeptical when:

  • The output contains specific statistics, dates, or quotes you didn't provide.
  • The writing is technically correct but sounds like nobody in particular wrote it.
  • The AI generated a "comprehensive" framework for a topic that actually requires deep expertise to address well.
  • The output is exactly what you asked for, but you're not sure it's actually right.
